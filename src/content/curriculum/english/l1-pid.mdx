Every controller level in Robavionix runs against the same standard fault library. L1 is where that story starts: a classical cascaded PID attitude controller, tuned by hand on two physically different airframes, pushed until each one breaks in its own way.

## Why start here

Most control-education failures aren't about content depth — they're about students crossing a math threshold without ever touching a real, verified plant. L1 assumes only transfer functions and frequency response. No state space, no Lyapunov functions. The goal is a working controller inside the first lab session on both airframes, and a precise, felt answer to a question that turns out to have two different answers: **how far can a PID controller be pushed before it gives up — and does that limit look the same on a quadrotor as it does on a fixed-wing aircraft?**

## What you build

On the quadrotor: a rate-loop PID on each axis, then an outer attitude loop cascaded on top, deployed to the STM32 flight controller board and exercised against the physical quadrotor testbed inside the HIL rig — not a pure simulation. On FT-1: the same rate + attitude cascade structure, designed against an AVL/Simulink aerodynamic model that's cross-checked two independent ways before a single gain gets tuned against it, then pressure-tested against the full nonlinear aircraft before anything gets called "working."

## The fault story

Every experiment in this level ends the same way: inject a fault from the standard library — degraded or jammed motor thrust on the quadrotor, a partial elevator or a jammed aileron on FT-1 — and record what happens. The point isn't to make PID fail dramatically — it's to give students a **precise, hands-on baseline** for the phrase "robustness margin," so that when L3 introduces sliding mode control against the same faults, the improvement is something they measured themselves, not something a lecture slide told them was true.
